Goals

The gols for this independent study was twofold. First, I wanted to familiarize myself with machine learning concepts and PyTorch through a hands on project. With the recent developments with transformer based LLMs, I wanted a deeper dive into the transformer architecture. Second, I wanted to apply the techniques in a Music Information Retrieval (MIR) task. As a novice electric guitarist, one of learning hurdles that I've yet to conquer is shaping the tone of the guitar with the use of an effects chain. Upon taking Natural Language Processing, I wanted to explore whether a statistical model could tackle the problem of audio effect parameter estimation.

Background

In music production, special audio effects can be applied to dry audio to modify timbre, pitch, time, and etc. to enhance musical expression or musicality of the audio. For electric guitars, this can be done with a pedal board, which is collection of pedals chained together into an effects chain. A pedal is a circuit designed to alter the incoming audio to create different effects, ranging from distortion, chorus, compression, reverb, and so on. Each pedal has set of parameters, usually in forms of physical knobs, that controls how and/or how much the pedal influence incoming signal. Figure 1 illustrates a simpiflied model.

The Problem


Figure 2. Problem of audio effects parameter estimation.

The problem I am trying to tackle with machine learning belongs to a broader set of problems called audio effects parameter estimation. In my case, I am specificallly interested in decoding both the sequence and parameters of pedals used given some wet signal.

Data

For training and evaluating models, I have used the EGFxSet and IDMT-SMT-Guitar Dataset. From both of the datasets, only the clean (no effects) recordings were used with the effects applied post hoc through Spotify's pedalboard. For monophonic data, playing of a single note was recorded. For polyphonic data, strumming of a single chord was recorded. While meta data for the recordings were provided, they were not utilized for final training/evaluation. Total of 5 effects were used: Compressor, Distortion, Chorus, Phaser, Reverb. Effects parameters were discretized into linear intervals. In regards to tokenization of pedalboards, unique token was dedicated per bins per parameters per effects. A separator token was used to delineate parameters of effects.


Figure 3. After loading the clean audio from the disk, a randomly generated pedalboard was applied to create the wet audio. Simultaneously, the generated pedalboard was tokenized.

Architecture

For the architecture, encoder-decoder model was chosen for its robust ability to map one sequence of tokens (audio) to another sequence of tokens (pedal tokens). See Figure 4. for the architecture layout. For the encoder, Descript Audio Codec's encoder was used. For the decoder, a standard transformer architecture was used with cross attention enabled. The decoder consisted of 8 transformer layers with 8 heads. Excluding DAC's encoder weights, there were total of 9.8 million trainable parameters. During training, the encoder's weights were frozen, only descending on the weights of the decoder.

Thoughts

There are many ways to improve the current design. Currently, quantization of the parameters naively split the range of the parameters in linear chunks. However, considering that human's audio perception is logarithmic, usage of logarithmic scale may help model learn representations that aligns with human perceptions. Secondly, tokenization procedure can be improved. As of now, it is not trivial to check the order of effects separate from their parameters. Thus, the model was never explictly rewarded for generating correct sequence of effects, which may have hurt model's ability to generalize.
Overall, this was an interesting project that helped me deepen my understanding of transformers (at least their architecture) and think about application of machine learning in music information retrieval.
